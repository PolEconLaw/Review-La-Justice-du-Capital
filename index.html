A note from the contributing authors

Interrogating Investment Disputes: Collective Reflections on Amina Hassani and Benjamin Lemoine’s Panel

This blog offers an anthology of reflections inspired by Amina Hassani and Benjamin Lemoine’s visit to Sciences Po. Challenged by Hassani and Lemoine’s multifaceted critique of arbitration’s foundational underpinnings, we each engage with one recurring theme raised during the event. Drawing on critical theory, case law, and, of course, Hassani and Lemoine’s remarks from the panel, we offer perspectives on the following facets of arbitration: the myth of neutrality, the public/private divide, hegemony, nationalization, the “substitution” of domestic systems, and possible reforms. Taken together, this blog hopes to capture the cacophony of questions and tensions that shape arbitration amidst what Hassani describes as a “crisis of legitimacy.”

The Myth of Neutrality

Jana McCloskey-Gholikhany

A central idea which stayed with me from Amina Hassani’s panel intervention was what she calls the “myth of neutrality”. Reading her book La Justice du Capital, I felt like she had put into words critiques I had already begun to have by confronting the content from my arbitration classes, often taught by arbitrators adopting a mainstream approach, to TWAIL scholarship. I arrived at the panel event with a list of questions on reforming the system and left with one much larger one: what if the system itself rests on a myth and is sustained by its critiques?

Arbitration and Democratic Exceptionalism

Amina Hassani’s panel intervention clarified that her critique is not simply that arbitration produces an unequal system which maintains the domination of the Global North over the Global South. It is more structural: arbitration is not democratic. Her book is sometimes misread as an argument that arbitration is the enemy of public interest, which sparks repeated questions on how it can be reformed to be more respectful of the environment and of human rights. This is not the core issue. Instead, arbitration operates in a form of legal exceptionalism. Certain disputes are framed as too technical or sensitive to be adjudicated by domestic courts. Therefore, they are displaced to a private system of dispute resolution, where parties choose their arbitrations and operate within a self-contained legal universe, external to democratic scrutiny. Though this displacement should seem shocking to the public, it is justified through the narrative of neutrality.

The Myth of Neutrality

The myth of neutrality refers to the widely accepted belief that international arbitration is neutral and fair.1 Like all myths, arbitration delivers a powerful narrative which spreads through repetition, even in the face of contradictory information. Historically, the narrative served to build the system by removing disputes from domestic courts in order to avoid the “biases” of national courts during the period of decolonisation. Proponents of arbitration promoted the system as an alternative form of dispute resolution which would provide a means for European countries to maintain their extractivist interests even after their colonies became independent. To achieve this goal, state sovereignty of newly independent countries needed to be challenged.

As Amina Hassani writes:1

« L’arbitrage était, de ce fait, considéré comme un instrument permettant la remise en cause continuelle de leur souveraineté, ce qui n’était pas sans leur rappeler certains fantômes du passé et auxquels l’arbitrage succédait. »

Sustaining the Myth Through Practice

For a myth to survive, it must be sustained through practice and belief. Firstly, the myth of neutrality of arbitration is maintained through the production of technical and technocratic knowledge.2 The legal scholarship on arbitration is dominated by practitioners in the field and generally presents arbitration as inherently neutral. As a result, the idea of neutrality becomes self-evident and displaces concerns onto issues which are framed as purely technical rather than structural, such as the publication of awards to improve transparency. Secondly, like with many myths, elder generations pass them down to the younger ones. In this sense, arbitration courses in law schools are frequently taught by arbitrators themselves which present the field as technical and competitive to gain access to. This shapes how a new generation of lawyers view arbitration, reinforcing a form of confirmation bias. Lastly, neutrality is constantly invoked in professional practice by arbitrators. This is illustrated through the language of tribunals: arbitration is framed as “delocalised justice” and tribunals are described as “independent” compared to biased domestic courts. As René Ureña notes, a common thread among arbitrators is the idea of local law as failed law.3 In contrast, arbitrations stand as a neutral alternative.

Another dimension of the myth is the opacity of arbitration. Knowledge of the system is confined to the bubble of the legal field and remains virtually invisible to the broader public, with the exception of the occasional case which attracts media attention.4 This lack of accessibility reinforces the idea that arbitration is very technical and complex, suggesting that only those with specialised knowledge are capable of understanding it. In doing so, it perpetuates the myth of neutrality as it shields arbitration from wider scrutiny and critique.

Reinforcing the Myth Through Critique

The most striking aspect of Amina Hassani’s argument is that criticism only serves to reinforce the myth of neutrality.1 Although Thomas’s contribution maps out five pillars for reforming the arbitral award, Amina Hassani argued during her intervention that discussions on reform only serve to legitimise the system. When asked during the panel event about her opinion on incorporating human rights into arbitration, through the means of counterclaims for instance, Hassani responded clearly that arbitration is structured to serve the interests of capital, not human rights. In this sense, reform debates shift the attention to technical fixes and sustain the myth of neutrality. In doing so, discussions on reform legitimise arbitration by treating it as necessary, and the more it is criticised the more it is reaffirmed.

A Final Question

At the end of the panel event, I asked Amina if she thought that the myth had been consciously constructed. Had the original proponents of arbitration during the period of decolonisation deliberately promoted the myth of neutrality to continuously maintain control over the resources of newly independent countries? Her answer was simply that we cannot know. That said, myths stay effective simply by being repeated and believed, even if they were not consciously designed to begin with…

Private Justice, Public Power

The Status of the Public/Private Divide in International Arbitration

Abby Goldman

I. Introduction

Hassani’s discussion on the fiction of “private justice” in arbitration nicely embroidered our previous discussion on the “capture” of the public by the private in international economic law. While we usually envisage the public (the demos) and the private (commercial interests) in separate spheres of influence, Hassani demonstrated how arbitration collapses this distinction. Technocrats with specialized training in the business sector, along with full-time practitioners in commercial law firms, argue and hear legal disputes before issuing binding legal decisions that can massively reshape a state’s public policy priorities. Accordingly, Hassani characterized this process as one that “is not democratic at all, and yet issues decisions that affect the demos.” In this respect, framing arbitration as purely “private justice” is fictitious. Rather, it is a private, capital-driven procedure that governs public outcomes.

II. Theoretical Reflections on the Public/Private Divide

Hassani’s argument finds strong support in critical theory. For example, certain global governance scholars encourage us to move beyond a binary understanding of public and private, and to think instead in terms of infrastructures that produce and serve different publics.1, 2 Viewed through this lens, the demos Hassani invokes — citizens in the host state whose public welfare is affected by investment arbitration decisions — can be understood as “thwarted publics”: groups precluded from disputing or participating in the process by nature of the investment infrastructure.3

The International Center for Investment Disputes (ICSID)’s holding in Metalclad Corp. v. United Mexican States illustrates this dynamic. Metalclad, a mining company, initiated ICSID proceedings against Mexico after officials in the San Luis Potosí region blocked its construction of a landfill in adherence with the state governor’s recent Ecological Decree declaring the area a protective zone for rare plant species.4

The tribunal sided with Metalclad. Noting that Mexico violated the fair and equitable treatment (FET) principle by failing to ensure “that all relevant legal requirements for the purpose of initiating, completing and successfully operating investments made, or intended to be made, under the Agreement should be capable of being readily known” and a “transparent and predictable framework for Metalclad’s business planning and investment,” the tribunal awarded Metalclad $16.7 million in compensation for costs incurred to build the landfill.5 As a result, investment principles like fair and equitable treatment sideline public interest and reconfigure state conduct around arrangements most legible to investors. The publics privileged by arbitration are ultimately limited to those whose interests can be articulated in the language of capital.

III. The Public/Private Divide in Practice

While critics of the public/private dichotomy readily proclaim its instability, practitioners are much more resistant to acknowledge its collapse. If we map the public/private dichotomy along Kennedy’s stages of the decline of the public/private distinction, arbitration practice is lodged in Stage 1: “hard cases with large stakes engage peoples’ energies in the task of manipulating, analyzing, and fretting about distinction.”6

The demarcation is also evident in how elite practitioners conceptualize and market the field. The law firm Reed Smith, for example, underscores that its lawyers are “embedded within industry groups,” capable of speaking “your sector’s language,” and “understand[ing] [of] its commercial drivers and how decisions play out in the project environment.”7 With the priority resting squarely on industry gains, regulatory and state policy concerns are framed as distant, separate risks to be managed rather than relevant interests to be considered.

One of the foundational pillars of investment arbitration foregrounds a similar logic. Aron Broches, principal architect of the ICSID Convention, has emphasized that the Convention aimed to “remove investment disputes from the intergovernmental political sphere” and to offer “a means of settling directly, on the legal plane, investment disputes between the State and foreign investor, which would insulate such disputes from the realm of politics and diplomacy.”8 The aim in practice, therefore, is to completely disembed arbitration from the public domain. While affected by the ICSID tribunal’s decisions, the interests of the demos are structurally excluded from the process.

IV. Conclusion

The persistence in framing public policy concerns as completely separate, mitigatable business risks is not accidental. Instead, it reflects deeper structural incentives to lessen the chance of being held accountable. If arbitration remains “private justice,” it needs only to answer to its precedent — not to the communities whose lives its decisions shape.

Hassani’s talk was an eye-opening debunk of the public/private divide, and a valuable lesson on the stakes of refusing to acknowledge its collapse. Only time will tell how much longer arbitration practitioners will succeed in keeping this fictional gap intact.

Arbitration as Domination

Maia Baciu

Amina Hassani’s indictment of arbitration as a technology of domination can be analyzed through the lenses of Bourdieu’s sociology of law, Andrea Leiter’s historical work on the making of international investment law, and critical analyses of neoliberal legality. We will unfold our review along Hassani’s three interconnected dimensions1: the social constitution of the arbitration field (I), the powers of arbitrators through characterisation, choice of law and valuation (II), and the elevation of market logic into a universal doctrinal benchmark (III).

I. The Social Field of Investment Arbitration

The first dimension of arbitration’s power of domination lies in the constitution of the field itself. Drawing on Bourdieu’s theory of fields, Hassani shows that international investment arbitration is not a neutral arena governed by technical expertise but a social field structured by the unequal distribution of symbolic capital among a small, tight-knit elite.2 The key mechanism is what Bourdieu calls multipositionnalité: the same individuals simultaneously occupy the roles of professor, legal counsel, and arbitrator, enabling a circular process of self-authorisation whereby doctrine, advocacy, and adjudication reinforce each other in a closed loop.

The paradigmatic figure here is Emmanuel Gaillard, who authored foundational doctrinal texts like the Fouchard Gaillard Goldman treatise on international commercial arbitration, developed a “transnational” vision of arbitration as an autonomous legal order, and then applied this very vision in his roles as arbitrator and counsel in some of the world’s largest investment disputes.

Leiter’s research confirms this pattern at the origin of the field. In her historical study of the field’s formation from 1922 to 1959,3 she shows how the same British international lawyers who drafted concession agreements in the mid-twentieth century subsequently arbitrated the resulting disputes. They also presented their arbitrary inventions as expressions of pre-existing universal law.

The authority of arbitral awards thus derives not from any external source of legitimacy (democratic mandate, legislative enactment, or even prior state consent in a meaningful sense), but from the constant repetition and normalization of arbitrary practices by the same actors occupying different positions within the field itself. Any novel claim, once recognized by a tribunal, is retroactively projected backward as an iteration of an allegedly pre-existing rule of law.4

The field also sustains itself through what Hassani analyses as a “front uni” against external criticism. Professor Thomas Clay’s recent op-ed in Le Monde5 defended investment arbitration as a bulwark of civilised peace against geopolitical disorder. This perfectly illustrates Bourdieu’s intérêt au désintéressement6: agents in a field will publicly defend the field in terms of universal benefit like peace and development, because the devaluation of the field would devalue their own accumulated capital within it.

II. Arbitrator Powers: Characterization, Applicable Law, Valuation

If the constitution of the field provides the sociological infrastructure for arbitration’s authority, the exercise of that authority in concrete disputes reveals its operational logic. The second axis of Hassani’s critique focuses on the powers exercised by arbitrators, specifically the power to characterise legal relationships, to select the applicable law, and to compensate. Presented as technical determinations, these operations are in reality profoundly political acts: the displacement of local legal orders, the transformation of political questions into technical matters of contract interpretation, and the methodological supremacy of market-based valuation.

First, the power to determine applicable law is exemplified by the 1951 Petroleum Development (Abu Dhabi) arbitration, in which Lord Asquith famously dismissed Islamic law as unfit for application to “modern commercial instruments” and instead invoked “principles rooted in the good sense and common practice of the generality of civilised nations,” which in substance reproduced English law.7 As Leiter observes, this reasoning rests on a double maneuver: first, a hierarchization of difference whereby non-Western legal orders are consigned to the past (“archaic,” “traditional”), while Western legal principles are positioned as uniquely capable of addressing modernity; second, a universalization of the particular, wherein English legal rules are presented not as the contingent product of a specific legal tradition but as the embodiment of universal reason itself.8

Second, the power of characterization proves equally potent. Hassani notes that investment tribunals have qualified sovereign debt securities as “investments” within the meaning of the ICSID Convention.9 Similarly, in the Lehman Brothers litigation, a ‘flip clause’ in a swap contract was characterised as a matter of contract in London but as a matter of bankruptcy in New York, the outcome of the dispute turning entirely on which category prevailed.10 In each case, the power to characterise is the power to determine outcomes, and the arbitrator exercises it under the cover of technical legal analysis. Viewed through Bourdieu’s lens, the award is a veredictum: an act of ontological promotion that created the legal reality it purported only to describe, making the renunciation of sovereign power “exist” through the sheer force of authoritative naming.11 Viewed through Leiter’s lens, the act of qualifying is a constitutive transformation that allows the tribunal to “speak the law” over matters that might otherwise remain within a state’s domestic jurisdiction.12 By qualifying debt as an 'investment' under ICSID, tribunals subject a state's attempt to reorganize its economy to international legal scrutiny, thereby constraining its sovereign power to restructure debt without incurring international liability.

Third, the power to evaluate compensation shows how the market is used as the ultimate benchmark, and investor expectations are treated as a protected legal right. As Hassani notes, arbitral tribunals rely on Fair Market Value (FMV) and Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) methodologies that compute compensation by projecting the stream of future profits an investor could have expected, resulting in colossal sums ($50 billion in Yukos v. Russia). According to Leiter, this reflects the shift from property protection to investment protection. Whereas property law is oriented toward safeguarding existing wealth, investment is inherently future-oriented, defined as “the commitment of resources with the goal of achieving a return.”13 This ideology transforms any state regulatory measure that diminishes “future profits” into a legal injury, cognisable by applying pseudo-neutral technical principles, regardless of the measure’s public purpose.14 These methodologies, as Hassani notes, are now applied even in the absence of any actual existing market, relying instead on hypothetical scenarios constructed by valuation experts.

III. The Critique-Recuperation Technique

When criticism of this system has become too visible to ignore, the response has been not to dismantle the architecture but to reform its surface. Boltanski and Chiapello’s central argument in The New Spirit of Capitalism is that capitalism survives because it continuously transforms itself, and it continues transforming itself by selectively integrating the critiques it faces without sacrificing accumulation.15

When arbitration was confronted with mounting critiques of opacity, exclusivity, and democratic deficit, the arbitral system did not resist these demands but selectively incorporated them, transforming a potentially delegitimizing challenge into a source of renewed justification. Hassani illustrates this with the introduction of amicus briefs in arbitration. They were admitted for the first time in Methanex Corp. v. United States (2001) and subsequently institutionalised in the 2006 ICSID Arbitration Rules (Rule 37(2)). Symbolically, it appeared as though the voice of the public had finally entered a private forum. Practically, an amicus brief has little impact on the arbitral tribunal, whose mandate remains the protection of the investment. Similarly, the introduction of transparency rules and the European Commission’s multilateral investment court project all represent what Hassani calls the “great adaptation of the justice of capital”16 and what Chiapello and Boltanski call the new spirit of capitalism: a series of corrective adjustments that absorb enough critique to secure the system’s legitimacy without ever contesting the foundational principle.

A Challenge to the Nationalization of Investment Disputes

Broad Subjectivity to U.S. Jurisdiction

Audrey Balliette

I. Introduction

Amina Hassani’s rejection of the infrastructure surrounding investment arbitration and her reluctance to endorse reforms to that system leave a number of questions as to what could replace and improve the international dispute resolution regime. The conversation in class alluded to two potential paths forward. The first path empowers the domestic systems of host states and would require bringing disputes to domestic courts and exhausting local remedies before any international recourse. The second path is one which has been approached during several lectures in this course, yet which no speaker has fully endorsed. It would entail a broad restructuring of the global economy, whereby formerly colonized states would demand (and build some mechanism to enforce) a reversal of the structural flow of capital from South to North via sweeping solutions such as reparations regimes, coordinated debt cancellation, and/or the creation of new democratized international financial institutions. Barring a comprehensive treatment of that second pathway, this response focuses on why the first is shortsighted, and indeed points to the necessity of the second set of solutions. In short, the alternative to international arbitration is not necessarily the host state’s domestic courts. This critique is not that host state courts are inadequate. It is that the alternative forum may not be the host state’s courts at all, but rather the creditor state’s domestic courts, which are considerably more powerful. As the cases below illustrate (several of which were assigned during this course), the legal infrastructure in the U.S. strongly favors recovery over sovereign immunity. As Benjamin Lemoine observes, this contractual subjection to creditor state jurisdictions represents “a form of informal imperialism, with its own codes and forces, which succeeds the formal imperialism of the 19th century.”1

II. FSIA and the Commercial Activity Exception

The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (“the FSIA”) governs when foreign states may be sued in U.S. federal courts.2 Originally enacted as a compromise between Congress and the Executive Branch to limit the historically broad grant of sovereign immunity that the U.S. had given to all foreign states, the FSIA has been subject to political influence from the start. The FSIA enumerates several exceptions to sovereign immunity which have broadened since its enactment. The commercial activity exception permits lawsuits against foreign states based on activity carried on in the United States or, most expansively, commercial activity abroad that causes a “direct effect” in the United States.3 The case that most infamously illustrates the breadth of this exception is Republic of Argentina v. Weltover. In that case, the Supreme Court held that Argentina’s unilateral rescheduling of bond payments caused a direct effect in the United States because payment was designated for New York, even where the bonds lacked other U.S. connections.4 Because dollar clearing passes through the U.S. financial system regardless of where parties are located, the jurisdictional hook of the “direct effect” prong extends to an enormous proportion of international commercial transactions. These include many disputes that arise entirely in a foreign state, including many of those governed by arbitration agreements.

The saga of the Argentina sovereign debt litigation further clarifies the stakes at hand. When NML Capital, a vulture fund that had purchased distressed Argentine bonds at a fraction of face value, obtained a U.S. judgment and sought enforcement, the Second Circuit’s pari passu ruling effectively gave a single federal district court judge the power to prohibit Argentina from servicing its restructured debt until holdouts were paid in full.5 This was the ruling that paralyzed Argentina’s debt payments. Although this dispute did not originate in investment arbitration, this litigation exploited New York’s role as governing law jurisdiction and shows how more powerful states strategically deploy private law tools.

III. Other Private Law Tools in the U.S. — Asset Attachment, RICO, and Extraterritorial Scope

U.S. jurisdiction over foreign sovereign conduct is not limited to the FSIA framework. Even when a dispute is resolved outside U.S. courts (i.e., in the host state’s own courts, or through arbitration), the enforcement phase may still pull the dispute into U.S. jurisdiction if the losing state has certain assets in the United States. The Crystallex litigation illustrates this dynamic. Crystallex, a Canadian mining company, obtained a $1.2 billion ICSID award against Venezuela for expropriation of its gold mining operations. The dispute initially lacked any connection to U.S. law. After Venezuela refused to pay, the Third Circuit held that PDVSA, Venezuela’s state oil company, was Venezuela’s alter ego, and allowed attachment of Citgo Petroleum, a U.S.-based subsidiary company with U.S. employees and operations, to satisfy the award.6 The practical result was that a foreign state’s sovereign commercial assets in the U.S. became vulnerable to seizure to satisfy an investment arbitration award, showing that the distance between a sovereign state and its commercial assets offers little protection.

Other tools of the U.S. legal system have also been deployed to pull cases into U.S. jurisdiction. In Chevron Corp. v. Donziger, Judge Kaplan used RICO to nullify an $8.6 billion Ecuadorian environmental judgment arising from oil contamination in the Amazon, decided in Ecuadorian courts, on the grounds that it had been obtained by fraud.7 Plaintiffs were left unable to enforce the judgment in any jurisdiction worldwide. Additionally, the U.S. has repeatedly enacted unilateral secondary sanctions using the dollar system and access to U.S. financial markets as the enforcement lever. Examples include the Helms-Burton Act,8 which created a private right of action in U.S. federal courts against any person trafficking in property confiscated by Cuba, and the Iran and Libya Sanctions Act9 of the same year, which imposed penalties on any foreign company investing in the Iranian and Libyan energy sectors, regardless of U.S. connections. This complex architecture shows that even when “powerful states put into effect laws that have an extraterritorial effect; third world States have little control.”10

IV. Conclusion

The core value underlying the argument by TWAIL scholars including Amina Hassani is that the distrust of Global South domestic courts is a colonial remnant. But Global South states re-asserting control and demanding domestic adjudication would still leave those states fully exposed to FSIA and similar litigation, both in the U.S. and abroad, especially as sovereign immunity is increasingly whittled away under a world moving away from international cooperation and respect for international law. Instead, “[i]maginative solutions are called for in the world of international law and institutions if the lives of the poor and marginal groups in the third and first worlds are to be improved.”11

Investment Arbitration, Grand Extralegal Solution to the Problem of "Parochial" National Legal Systems

Dillon Lerach

I. Introduction

During both Amina Hassani’s intervention and other course sessions, our course has repeatedly exposed and investigated a conceptual tug-of-war between a supranational contract law that exists outside the control of any national state (termed lex mercatoria) and national courts, which large companies seize to recognize and enforce the awards rendered by self-selected tribunals that operate, intra muros, outside of any nation’s legal system.

Whether using complex transnational subsidiaries and spinoff shells to isolate the American “Names” during the Lloyd’s scandal,1 whether relying on their status as corporations — and their designation as juridical, rather than natural, persons — to avoid liability under existing legal regulations like the Alien Tort Statute,2 or whether levying public law instruments (namely investment treaties) to enforce private claims that arose against other private actors against states,3 these multinational, sophisticated companies exploit the grey zone between public and private law and between national and international law, evading responsibility and regulation before national courts while levying these very same organs of public justice to execute their privately arbitrated awards — further extracting capital from post-colonial states to metropolitan private companies.

And yet this distinctly non-national system of arbitration is built on a foundation of tolerance and enthusiasm by national courts: legal systems around the world encourage parties to derogate from their power of adjudication and to escape their regulatory ambit. Indeed, national courts dismiss the private citizens who, in their concern and outrage, attempt to levy the existing legal instruments devised to reign in this type of corporate recklessness and lawless behavior. Courts overwhelmingly take the opportunity to reduce their regulatory scope, clear their dockets, and throw up their hands, minimizing and disparaging existing laws and rights of action as “parochial” vestiges of the non-internationalized legal system, which, despite clear text, unambiguous legislative intent, or centuries of common-law use, they consign to the dustbin of history to make way for the increased profit, efficiency, and international exchange promised by the self-regulating world of lex mercatoria contract law. This is the explicit rationale of many of the court decisions upholding the move to an ICSID-dominated, lex mercatoria-governed system of hit-and-run extraction by large companies against states that have rendered themselves helpless.

Investment Arbitration, an essential tool of the lex mercatoria, is a method of securing the flow of capital from former colony to former metropole. This transfer is then legitimated by national courts by converting arbitral awards to judgments, and then executed by them, backed by their police power and recourse to force — all while these national courts have divested themselves of the power to examine virtually any injustice or irregularity in the proceedings.4 Having escaped the confines of any state’s legal system, these companies are loosed upon the world, and return to a system of pure extraction against the global periphery, which is coerced to consent to one-way investor arbitration clauses in Bilateral Investment Treaties (BITs). These corporations do not depend on a properly functioning free market: they have levied, as private parties, the tools of public international law and wield them against (largely global-south) states.

II. From “Fossil” to Capital — ICSID as Mechanism of Extraction

“Ivory? I should think so. Heaps of it, stacks of it. The old mud shanty was bursting with it. You would think there was not a single tusk left either above or below the ground in the whole country. ‘Mostly fossil,’ the manager had remarked, disparagingly. It was no more fossil than I am; but they call it fossil when it is dug up.” — Joseph Conrad, Heart of Darkness (emphasis added)

Amina Hassani and Benjamin Lemoine have examined how ICSID and its regime of investment arbitration is a continuation of state-backed private capitalism’s effort to extract “fossils” from the former colonies of the global south. ICSID originally was built on the extraction of “fossils” from the colonial sphere, and they still undergird the motivation behind the growth and enforcement of the investor arbitration system, whether in the form of oil and petrochemicals in Venezuela and Iran today,5 or ivory and rubber under the legal régime of the Congo Free State a century ago.6 There has been a transformation, though, whereby private actors, rather than setting sail for the colonies to bring back gold, oil, coal, and rubber, now extract capital itself from the nominally independent states of the Global South. In the wake of the Argentine Debt Crisis of 2001–2002, investors brought a plethora of claims — the most ever directed against a single state — against Argentina.7

Rather than using arbitration and treaty law to transfer physical resources, private litigants could now directly transfer the capital of states after suing them as peers under the auspices of BITs. Further placing states on the same level as investors, national courts have been eager to waive debtor states’ sovereign immunity defenses in the face of litigation by vulture funds. Donegal International Ltd. v. Zambia, 1 Lloyd’s Rep 397 (Q.B. 2007) (a state was not entitled to state immunity in respect of a claim to enforce a settlement agreement relating to sovereign debt). In the United States, similar developments have severely curtailed sovereign immunity defenses by foreign states following the passage of the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act. This system “impos[es] the idea that trade is above all a private matter and that when it becomes involved in transactions with private parties, the state must agree to play this contractual game on equal terms.”8 Sovereign debt has become contractualized under the new lex mercatoria, rather than being treated as a matter of the public fisc. This contractualization is supported not only by BITs and the ICSID system, but is further reinforced by the legal instruments of rich developed states where enforcement of the collection of these debts is sought, in particular in the United States and United Kingdom.

After a number of controversial cases brought by investors against states in the global south, investment arbitration has increasingly found itself facing a crisis of legitimacy. Citizens have become increasingly outraged by awards rendered following measures enacted by governments to maintain the public welfare. Compare, e.g., Philip Morris Asia Ltd. v. Commonwealth of Australia, UNCITRAL, PCA Case No. 2012-12 (dismissing expropriation claim following cigarette plain packaging regulation for lack of jurisdiction) with Philip Morris Brands Sàrl, Philip Morris Products S.A. and Abad Hermanos S.A. v. Oriental Republic of Uruguay, ICSID Case No. ARB/10/7 (granting jurisdiction but dismissing claims on the merits under public health and ordre public defenses). In order to rebut some of the most striking objections to ICSID arbitration, the United Nations General Assembly passed the Mauritius Convention on Transparency, though these reforms have only been ratified by nine states and the Council of Europe.9 Even if the convention gains wider adoption, corporations have already begun to turn back to less exposed instruments, such as contracts, rather than the increasingly infamous BITs that would be covered by any reform.10 Companies have long relied on public international law to enforce their will against poor states, but the private-public grey zone allows them to shift back to instruments of private law as it benefits them.

Benjamin Lemoine’s analysis — Mauritius Convention and certain countervailing caselaw notwithstanding — is correct: ICSID’s investment arbitration should be seen as a tool of extraction. Under its aegis not only are some countries reduced to places of extraction of minerals and resources (fossil), but the very form of dispute resolution imposed on them is itself extractive. Investment arbitration is a part of what financial capitalism already does: use states and their recourse to force as a safety net to collect on judgments. Furthermore, it is doubtful that efforts to restrict BITs or to impose transparency and reporting requirements on ICSID tribunals will have much structural effect, as creditors still will have recourse to powerful aspects of contract law, selectively enforced by the national courts of rich, developed states, to transfer capital out of the post-colonial global south.

III. “A State in the Disguise of A Merchant”: Pirates, Extraction, and Legal Recourse on The New High Seas

Law is divided between the public and the private, and yet what falls on which side at the intersection of the two? With ICSID’s foundation on instruments of public international law, elevating private corporations to peers of the states in which they invested before bringing breach of contract claims, the distinction becomes muddled. This turbidity, so essential to the ICSID-and-lex mercatoria legal regime, is not new. Indeed, for centuries the tools of extraction from the colonial sphere have “blurred the line” between public and private law: the two great historical examples of loot (a Hindustani word adopted by the British East India Company during its conquest of Bengal),11 the British East India Company and the Congo Free State, occurred when private corporations, protected by the rights guaranteed to persons — both natural and juridical — took on public functions and were loosed beyond the national territory and the national courts’ effective jurisdiction, only to be bailed out by the public fisc when they encountered bankruptcy-inducing losses.12

The British East India Company — in the lightly rephrased words of Edmund Burke: a beast at once public and private, immune from suit, backed by its own army and coining its own money, yet but a private juridical person under the protection of the King and Empire — inhabited a grey zone of public and private law that allowed it, a legal person, to evade the state’s courts and legal system when they sought to regulate it and to seize them when beneficial, such as when it needed access to public funds to stave off insolvency.13 This is just as companies today exploit the liminal public-private space afforded to them by BITs, approaching states as peers under international contract law, yet avoiding liability as private juridical persons and simultaneously enjoying rights granted to private citizens. In short, they have the best of both worlds. Contemporary thinkers like Gunther Teubner grapple with the problem of holding fictitious or “moral” persons like the British East India Company and other modern multinational corporations accountable for their actions (German law largely eschews the concept of corporate liability or wrongdoing),14 but rather than requiring a novel solution, the Anglo-American Common Law has had tools to regulate such extraterritorial, extranational behavior since the founding of the American Republic.

The Alien Tort Statute, passed as part of the First Judiciary Act of 1789, reads: “The district courts shall have original jurisdiction of any civil action by an alien for a tort only, committed in violation of the law of nations or a treaty of the United States.”15 This grant of jurisdiction was ruled constitutionally valid under Congress’s enumerated power to “define and punish Piracies and Felonies on the high Seas, and Offences against the Law of Nations.”16 Volumes have been written about the statute’s somewhat opaque legislative history, but after two centuries of quasi-dormancy, the ATS and its power to regulate extraterritorial conduct by a foreign corporation came before the Supreme Court of the United States in Kiobel v. Dutch Royal Petroleum Co., 569 U.S. 108 (2013). Kiobel’s first round of briefing centered over whether a corporation could be held liable in tort under the Law of Nations, and amici pointed out that while there were not clear precedents under customary international law, general principles of law provided historical precedents under which courts decided to exercise jurisdiction against the British East India Company.17 Though they are rare, they provided a clear ground to regulate multinational corporate behavior that would otherwise fall through the cracks between national legal systems, so long as that conduct rendered the tortfeasor — like the pirate and slave trader before him — hostis humani generis, an enemy of all mankind.18 Kiobel was reargued and decided on touch and concern grounds, largely drawing from the earlier-decided Morrison v. National Australia Bank, 561 U.S. 247 (2010) (Scalia, J.) (dismissing claims arising outside the United States brought under SEC Rule 10b-5).19 These cases came in as part of a mounting crest of Supreme Court decisions limiting the prescriptive and jurisdictional reach of U.S. law; see, for example, Nabisco’s limiting of RICO’s private right of action and Mitsubishi’s delegation of Sherman antitrust claims to private, party-selected arbitrators. 579 U.S. __ (2016); 473 U.S. 614 (1985). The Court eventually refused to exercise prescriptive jurisdiction over multinational corporations despite clear analogues in the cases brought against the British East India Company and the Alien Tort Statute’s legislative history. Jesner v. Arab Bank PLC, 584 U.S. 241 (2018).

With the rise of lex mercatoria thinking, courts have severely curtailed long-existing legal tools that regulated and controlled predatory behavior by multinational corporations operating on the metaphorical high seas.20 Indeed, again and again courts sitting in admiralty21 struck back at domestic legal protections in favor of “present-day commercial realities and expanding international trade.” The Bremen v. Zapata, 407 U.S. 1, 15 (1972). The courts explicitly justify their retreat from their fundamental role, which they were created by Congress to perform, on the grounds that “The expansion of American business and industry [would] hardly be encouraged if, notwithstanding solemn contracts, we insist[ed] on a parochial concept that all disputes must be resolved under our laws and in our courts.” Id. at 9. The Court repeated with no elaboration the same fiction attacked by Hassani and Lemoine, that enforcement of Lex Mercatoria norms “accord[ ] with ancient concepts of freedom of contract, and reflect[ ] an appreciation of the expanding horizons of American contractors who seek business in all parts of the world.” Id. at 11. Any reliance on centuries of common-law precedent or jurisdictional text was dismissed as “hardly more than a vestigial legal fiction … [that] … has little place in an era when all courts are overloaded and when businesses, once essentially local, now operate in world markets … [and] … reflects something of a provincial attitude …” Id. at 12.

American law’s retreat from global regulation is not only concomitant with the rise of global value chains governed by a lex mercatoria contract law unmoored from the laws of states, the retreat was necessary for the rise to occur and was explicitly justified on those grounds. American courts, whether on grounds of comity, present-day commercial realities, or plain hostility to plaintiffs’ cases, have refused to extend the protection and force of American law across borders. There is a countervailing hope, though, that American Law still has a role in regulating the frontier (as contrasted with border by Hans Lindahl); there is still enthusiasm for extraterritorial application of personal jurisdiction when it comes to Fuld v. Palestine Liberation Organization, 606 U.S. __ (2025). Amina Hassani and Benjamin LeMoine have correctly identified the colonial antecedents of the contemporary anti-statist push in legal thought and its basis on extraction from periphery to metropole. A reinvigorated transnational common law remains a more viable solution than ICSID or other softlaw tools (risk of Conflicting Judgments, as experienced by Steven Donzinger, aside). “Our system of remedial law resembles an old Gothic castle, erected in the days of chivalry, but fitted up for a modern inhabitant. The moated ramparts, the embattled towers, and the trophied halls, are magnificent and venerable, but useless, and therefore neglected”22 until times change such that they must once again have their day and come to the fore.

International Arbitration in the Shadow of Finitude

An Indispensable Law in Search of Redemption

Thomas Charnotet

Today, no one can do without international arbitration. In an interconnected world where capital flows defy borders and transnational disputes abound, from mining contracts to energy infrastructure projects, arbitration remains irreplaceable. Rooted in the party autonomy of Article II of the 1958 New York Convention and Article 25 of the 1965 ICSID Convention, it offers unique cross-border enforceability (169 States Parties to the NYC), cutting-edge technical expertise, and procedural flexibility that state courts struggle to match. Yet, under the low, heavy sky of a globalized capitalism that is brutally discovering its material, ecological, and normative limits, this legal beacon is faltering, cracked by the storms of resource scarcity and the imperatives of planetary transition.

Amina Hassani, in her seminal work The Justice of Capital (La Fabrique éditions, 2025), dissects this foundational illusion: neutrality is not an ontological given, but a constructed performance, eroded by transnational public order (Art. V(2)(b) NYC) and investor-state asymmetries. Benjamin Lemoine (CNRS) expands on this with an analysis of “finitude capitalism”: arbitration governs the scarcity of minerals (lithium, cobalt), imposing through FET (Art. 3 ILO) or indirect expropriation (Art. 5 ILO) a capitalist law in defiance of the Paris Agreement (Art. 2(1)(a)).

The Fractures of the Present: Legitimacy, Asymmetry, and Regulatory Paralysis

Arbitration today is buckling under an accumulation of contradictions so massive and so unnatural that it threatens its very existence: systemic opacity, chronic and quasi-institutionalized conflicts of interest, glaring structural asymmetry between the offensive investor and the defensive state, and a paralyzing brake on public regulations in the general interest. This pathological drift reaches its peak in the case law: Philip Morris v. Australia (PCA, 2015, para. 570), which transformed a public health measure strictly compliant with Article 11 of the WHO Framework Convention into indirect expropriation under the FET (Australia-Hong Kong BIT, Art. 3(2)), sanctifying the investor’s “legitimate expectations” in defiance of health sovereignty (Tecmed v. Mexico, ICSID, 2003, para. 149).

Worse still, RWE Innogy GmbH and Uniper Benelux NV v. the Netherlands (ICSID, 2024 under TCE) pits the mandatory decarbonization of the European Green Deal (EU Regulation 2021/1119) directly against the contractual stability of coal-fired power plants, elevating fossil fuel investments to an untouchable dogma in opposition to the global ecological imperative. This static and rigid body of case law blatantly disregards the evolving interpretation of treaties enshrined in Article 31(3)(c) of the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT) (Eiser Infrastructure v. Italy, ICSID Annulment, 2019, para. 191), paralyzing public action in the name of an excessively narrow doctrine of police powers (Methanex v. United States, UNCITRAL, 2005, Part IV.D).

The result is thus perverse: an instrument created to facilitate global trade becomes an obstacle to the vital ecological transition, protecting obsolete assets at the expense of future generations and the emerging transnational public order (Paris Agreement, CBD 1992, UNGA Res. 77/297). Hassani and Lemoine demonstrate this: this capitalist hypertrophy undermines arbitral neutrality to the point of absurdity, transforming the arbitrator into an unwitting guardian of a finite capitalism that defies the very physics of our planet.

Toward a Renewed Approach to Arbitration: Five Doctrinal Pillars for the Post-Extractive Era

First Pillar: Mandatory Sustainability Clauses and Compensatory Loss Sharing

The first essential reform lies in the systematic inclusion, within every bilateral investment treaty (BIT) or international investment contract, of a mandatory sustainability clause stating that “any government measure consistent with multilateral climate commitments, notably Article 4 of the Paris Agreement and Article 8 of the Convention on Biological Diversity, takes precedence over economic stability clauses such as umbrella clauses or freezing clauses, subject to proportionate compensation calculated via a loss-sharing mechanism indexed to CO₂ emissions avoided and certified by an independent third party appointed by the UNFCCC.”

This approach, already tested in nuce in Rockhopper Italia S.p.A. v. Italy (ICSID, Award, August 23, 2022, para. 598), where the Tribunal rejected the claim for compensation by invoking the doctrine of police powers, elevates the public interest to the status of a peremptory norm within the meaning of Article 53 of the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT). It thus reconciles the fair and equitable treatment (FET) standard with the requirements of international public order, while maintaining substantial protection for the investor through a strengthened proportionality test as theorized by Wälde & Kolo (« Environmental Regulation in BITs », 2007).

Legally, this clause enshrines the progressive interpretation of treaties required by Article 31(3)(c) of the VCLT at the heart of modernized BITs (2012 UNCITRAL Model, Art. 20), making any award that contravenes it subject to annulment for manifest error regarding the applicable rule (Art. 52(1)(b) of the ICSID Convention) or refusal of enforcement for contravention of public policy (Art. V(2)(b) of the NYC). Its utility is clear: it prevents regulatory paralysis without depriving the investor of an effective remedy, restoring a contractual balance suited to the era of finitude.

Second Pillar: Active Transparency and Binding Precedential Review

The crisis of legitimacy in arbitration, fueled by its chronic lack of transparency, necessitates making transparency a cornerstone of due process in arbitration. It is therefore appropriate to establish a centralized platform, the “ICSID Open Awards Hub,” tasked with systematically publishing anonymized awards relating to critical resources or climate disputes, accompanied by comprehensive metadata specifying the amount awarded (Art. 42 ICSID Convention), the FET grounds relied upon (CMS v. Argentina, para. 279) and the environmental clauses invoked (Art. 10(2) TCE).

Concurrently, a triennial precedential review conducted by a multinational committee of experts (UN, OECD, UNEP) would impose binding effect on future awards under penalty of partial res judicata (Art. 41 ICSID Rules 2022), this effect being enforceable both in annulment proceedings (Art. 52 ICSID) and in refusal of exequatur (Art. V(1)(e) NYC). Drawing inspiration from the UNCITRAL Rules on Transparency in Treaties (Mauritius Convention, 2014, Art. 3), this reform establishes democratic legitimacy through public scrutiny, transforming traditional opacity into a strictly justified and proportionate exception, akin to the ECHR Salduz v. Turkey (2008) test applied by analogy.

It serves a dual purpose: on the one hand, it combats public mistrust by establishing consistent case law without making arbitration overly rigid; on the other hand, it preserves the fundamental principle of party autonomy (Art. II(1) NYC) and trade secrets (EU Directive 2016/943), offering a measured response to contemporary criticisms.

Third Pillar: Expanded Counterclaims and a Two-Phase Procedure to Ensure True Symmetry

The structural asymmetry between investors and states, which lies at the heart of the criticisms leveled against arbitration, requires procedural pluralism reinforced by the systematic authorization of amici curiae (Art. 37 of the 2022 ICSID Rules), state counterclaims (Art. 46 ICSID), and expanded standing for actio popularis in cases of serious violations of mandatory ESG standards (UN Guiding Principles 2011, Princ. 11–24).

For disputes involving critical resources, a two-phase procedure is required: an initial phase of sovereign determination of societal and environmental harm conducted by an independent ad hoc panel (PCA Rules for Environmental Disputes, 2023, Art. 28), with limited evidentiary weight; followed by a second phase of bilateral arbitration on the amount of damages, with interim cost-sharing (Art. 42 ICSID). The 2022 ICSID reform (arbitral terms limited to 8 years, revised Art. 14(3)) and the European Multilateral Investment Tribunal (Proposal CL 2016/0287, Art. 3) institutionalize this hybridization, breaking the asymmetry through an extensive pro homine interpretation of ICSID Article 25, while scrupulously safeguarding due process (Kardassopoulos v. Georgia, Annulment, 2010, para. 185).

This approach is effective because it restores the balance of power within the arbitral tribunal, involves affected third parties, and directly addresses allegations of bias, without undermining the principle of equality of arms.

Fourth Pillar: Strengthened Arbitral Ethics and ESG Certification

Recurring conflicts of interest and the phenomenon of “double hats” undermine perceived arbitral neutrality, making mandatory ESG certification of arbitrators essential (ICC Task Force on Sustainable Arbitration, 2024), accompanied by a minimum portfolio of 30% “green” cases and annual training in UNFCCC customary law (Paris Rulebook, 2018) and TCFD disclosures (FSB, 2023).

A comprehensive public registry of appointments (extension of the IBA Guidelines on Conflicts of Interest, Art. 12) and a term limit of eight years would prevent such abuses, as these safeguards are enforceable in a challenge for lack of independence (Art. 14(1) ICSID; Perenco v. Ecuador, Challenge Decision, 2011). Anchored in a strengthened due process (Art. V(1)(b) NYC), this pillar transforms neutrality into verifiable performance, in line with the requirements outlined by Amina Hassani during the conference. Its value lies in restoring credible impartiality, essential to the institution’s survival in the face of accusations of collusion, without introducing a rigidity incompatible with arbitral flexibility.

Fifth Pillar: Hybrid Mediation and Dispute Prevention Funds

Finally, the establishment of a “Green Arbitration Track” (SIAC Rules 2025, Appendix 2) requiring a mandatory 90-day pre-arbitration mediation (Art. 17 UNCITRAL Mediation Rules, 2002), accompanied by escalation clauses to arbitration and the appointment of a neutral climate evaluator mandated by UNCITRAL (Art. 6), would help defuse early-stage disputes.

A multilateral dispute prevention fund (World Bank) would cover 50% of arbitration costs in the event of mediation failure, with this coverage contingent upon a measurable reduction in post-dispute emissions (independent ISO 14064 audit), accompanied by a cost-shifting penalty in the event of non-compliance. In accordance with the UNCITRAL Notes on Mediation (A/RES/57/101), this mechanism preserves the effectiveness of arbitration while promoting a transitional consensus. It proves particularly useful in the era of finitude, as it anticipates conflicts related to scarcity (Lemoine’s thesis) and combines arbitration and mediation for the sustainable governance of the commons.

Epilogue: The Reform of Arbitration — Neither Abolition Nor Sacralization

International arbitration, the modern culmination of jus honorarium, reveals itself in the Anthropocene as a philosophically tragic institution: a dialectic between the contractualist voluntas of capital (party autonomy, Art. II NYC) and the ratio superior of planetary limits, where the unnatural accumulation it undergoes, opacity as a concealment of democratic doxa, asymmetry as a Rawlsian negation of equitable reciprocity, undermines its very legitimacy (Hassani). Faced with this aporia, its redemption requires a Kelsenian refoundation: moving beyond static ILO positivism toward an evolving Grundnorm (Art. 31(3)(c) VCLT), where the arbitrator, an Aristotelian phronimos, strikes the right balance between private dominium and ecological imperium.

Neither abolition nor sacralization: post-finitude arbitration will embody a mature private international law, Kantian in its cosmopolitics, Hegelian in its synthesis of contradictions, guardian of the planetary commons against extractive hubris, thereby fulfilling its original promise: pacta sunt servanda in the service not of unlimited flux, but of revealed finitude.

But the question remains the same: are the states and the parties ready to take this plunge?
